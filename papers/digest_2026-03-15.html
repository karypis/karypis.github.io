#1 — Highly Innovative (85)

Optimal Expert-Attention Allocation in Mixture-of-Experts: A Scalable Law for Dynamic Model Design

Junzhuo Li, Peijie Jiang, Changxin Tian, Jia Liu, Zhiqiang Zhang, Xuming Hu · arxiv.org · Recommended articles
Why this rank: This paper scores 85 because it identifies and rigorously characterizes a previously unmodeled first-order scaling variable—the expert-attention FLOPs ratio—that is fundamental to MoE architecture design. The controlled experimental methodology cleanly isolates the internal allocation effect across scales and sparsity regimes, yielding a closed-form power-law relationship with strong out-of-sample generalization to held-out sparsity levels. The practical implications are significant: this gives MoE designers principled guidance where only heuristics existed before.
Show full summary & analysis
Problem & Motivation: Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures allow large language models to scale parameter count without proportionally increasing per-token computation. However, a critical design question has been largely ignored: how should compute be distributed *internally* between attention sublayers and expert (feed-forward) sublayers? In practice, this expert-attention FLOPs ratio is inherited from dense Transformer designs or tuned heuristically, despite expert layers often dominating compute budgets at scale. Existing scaling laws (Kaplan et al., Chinchilla) prescribe optimal balances between parameters and training tokens but implicitly assume a fixed internal allocation, leaving the expert–attention trade-off unmodeled. This paper addresses that gap. Methodology: The authors define r = C_E / C_A as the FLOPs ratio of expert compute to attention compute, and sparsity S = (E - E_act) / E as the fraction of inactive experts per token. They conduct controlled sweeps over r across multiple model scales (100M–5B total parameters, up to 1×10²¹ training FLOPs) and four sparsity levels (S ∈ {82.35%, 90.91%, 95.38%, 97.67%}, corresponding to E ∈ {17, 33, 65, 129} total experts), using GPT-style decoder-only MoE Transformers trained on a multilingual/multimodal dataset. By fixing per-token compute budget C_token = (C_A + C_E) / n_token and varying r in [0.2, 1.5], they isolate the internal allocation effect from confounding factors. The empirical optimal r* is extracted from loss minima, then fitted to a power-law r* = α_r · C^{β_r}, where the coefficients themselves follow power-law relationships with sparsity: α_r = 6.7×10⁻⁵(1−S)^{−1.23} and β_r = 0.24(1−S)^{0.21}. This allocation law is then incorporated into an extended Chinchilla-style loss model: L = a/N^α + b/D^β + c·e^R(1−S)^γ/N^λ + d·r/(r+1) + τ, fitted via L-BFGS with 9 parameters. Key Results: The optimal FLOPs ratio r* is empirically stable and well-defined (a clear valley in the loss surface, not noise), increases monotonically with total compute across all sparsity regimes, and is strongly modulated by sparsity. Specifically, low-sparsity models (more experts activated per token) favor higher r* at scale, while high-sparsity models grow r* more gradually. The fitted coefficients for the extended scaling law (Table 1: α=0.6288, β=0.0453, λ=0.4228, γ=0.0431, τ=13.74, a=15.12, b=18.62, c=39.55, d=0.0499) achieve strong agreement on held-out data, including a held-out sparsity level (S=97.67%) excluded entirely from fitting—demonstrating out-of-sample generalization. Compared to prior MoE scaling formulations (Wang et al. 2024, Abnar et al. 2025), the proposed law better captures loss trends across both small and large models and across high-sparsity regimes where prior methods fail. Strengths: - Identifies and rigorously characterizes a previously unmodeled first-order scaling variable (expert-attention FLOPs ratio) with practical, closed-form guidance for MoE design - Controlled experimental design (fixed per-token compute, sweep over r) cleanly isolates the internal allocation effect across a broad range of scales and sparsity regimes - Extended scaling law generalizes out-of-sample to held-out sparsity levels, validating the formulation's predictive power beyond the training distribution Weaknesses / Limitations: - Analysis is restricted to autoregressive language modeling; generalization to multimodal tasks, non-causal architectures, or adaptive (learned) routing is unvalidated - Hardware-level constraints such as communication costs in distributed training and memory bandwidth effects are not accounted for, which can substantially alter the practical optimal r in real deployments - Experiments use a fixed activation pattern (3 experts per token: 1 shared + 2 routed) and fixed context length (4096); the sensitivity of the discovered scaling laws to these architectural choices is unexplored

#2 — Highly Innovative (83)

Matching Features, Not Tokens: Energy-Based Fine-Tuning of Language Models

Samy Jelassi, Mujin Kwun, Rosie Zhao, Yuanzhi Li, Nicolo Fusi, Yilun Du, Sham M. Kakade, Carles Domingo-Enrich · arxiv.org · Recommended articles
Why this rank: This paper scores 83 because it introduces a genuinely novel fine-tuning objective that addresses the fundamental train/test distribution mismatch of teacher forcing without requiring any task-specific reward signal or verifier. The theoretical connection to energy-based models and proper scoring rules is elegant, and the practical result of simultaneously improving accuracy, cross-entropy, and distributional calibration — breaking the accuracy/calibration tradeoff inherent to RLVR — is surprising and significant. The approach opens a new design axis for LLM fine-tuning applicable in non-verifiable settings where RL methods cannot operate.
Show full summary & analysis
Problem & Motivation: Standard supervised fine-tuning (SFT) via cross-entropy (CE) under teacher forcing optimizes next-token prediction on ground-truth prefixes, but at deployment the model must condition on its own generated outputs. This train/test distribution mismatch causes compounding errors over long sequences — empirically, a conditional feature-matching (CFM) loss (measuring how well a model's rollout distribution matches the data distribution in embedding space) grows monotonically with completion length even for low-perplexity SFT models. Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) addresses behavioral calibration but requires a task-specific correctness signal and degrades validation cross-entropy, representing a fundamental accuracy/calibration tradeoff. The paper targets the gap left by both: a fine-tuning objective that improves sequence-level distributional calibration without requiring any reward or verifier. Methodology: The authors introduce Energy-Based Fine-Tuning (EBFT), which directly optimizes a feature-matching loss: the squared L2 distance between the mean feature embedding of model rollouts and that of ground-truth completions, where features are extracted from a frozen copy of the base model (concatenating intermediate activations at depths 25%, 50%, 75%, normalized per block). Because this loss cannot be estimated directly from (context, ground-truth) pairs alone, they derive an unbiased REINFORCE estimator by sampling n completions per context and computing per-rollout rewards with an RLOO (leave-one-out) baseline. Each reward has an alignment term (inner product of rollout features with ground-truth features) and a diversity term (negative mean inner product across rollout pairs). Training efficiency is achieved via strided block-parallel rollout generation using a custom attention mask (from Quiet-STaR), which amortizes prefix computation and enables batched feature extraction across multiple anchored contexts from a single sequence. Feature whitening via the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse of the empirical second-moment matrix is applied to handle anisotropy and improve conditioning. The practical objective mixes the feature-matching loss with a small CE regularization coefficient γ ≥ 0 to stabilize cross-entropy. Under KL regularization, EBFT is shown to implicitly define an energy-based model whose optimal policy takes the form of an exponential tilt of the base distribution — connecting feature matching to classical energy-based modeling and proper scoring rules. Key Results: EBFT is evaluated on Q&A coding (OpenCodeInstruct, Qwen2.5-1.5B), unstructured coding (SwallowCode), and translation (ALMA, Llama-3.2-1B). On Q&A coding, EBFT achieves greedy HumanEval 0.548 vs. SFT 0.483 and pass@16 0.771 vs. 0.728 for SFT, matching RLVR (0.535 greedy, 0.752 pass@16) without any correctness signal. EBFT achieves the best feature-matching loss across all methods (0.258 vs. 0.315 SFT and 0.442 RLVR), and the best validation CE (0.207 vs. 0.289 SFT; RLVR degrades to 0.774). On unstructured coding where RLVR is inapplicable, EBFT outperforms SFT on all pass@k metrics (pass@16: 0.769 vs. 0.747). On translation, EBFT achieves the highest COMET scores (greedy WMT22: 0.725 vs. SFT 0.717 and RLVR 0.697) and the lowest CE (1.670 vs. SFT 1.782). Feature-matching improvements generalize beyond the 8-token training horizon to completion lengths up to 64 tokens. Ablations confirm that last-token pooling with whitening is critical; random-weight feature networks hurt only modestly; scaling the feature network from 1.5B to 7B yields no additional gains. EBFT improvements are consistent across model scales (1.5B, 3B, 7B) with no sign of diminishing returns. Strengths: - Addresses a genuine fundamental limitation of teacher-forced training (distribution shift at inference) with a principled, theoretically grounded objective connected to proper scoring rules and energy-based models. - Requires no task-specific reward, verifier, or preference data, making it applicable in non-verifiable settings where RLVR is unavailable — demonstrated concretely on raw-code continuation. - Simultaneously improves downstream task accuracy, validation cross-entropy (better than SFT despite not optimizing CE directly), and distributional calibration — avoiding the accuracy/calibration tradeoff inherent to RLVR. Weaknesses / Limitations: - Significantly higher compute cost than SFT: a single EBFT epoch takes ~36 hours on two 80GB H100s (unoptimized, without vLLM), compared to 0.5 hours for SFT, due to on-policy rollout generation and feature network forward passes. - Evaluated only on models up to 7B parameters with short rollout horizons (G=4–8 tokens); scaling behavior for longer rollouts or much larger models is unknown, and the frozen feature network may become a bottleneck at larger scales.

#3 — Highly Innovative (82)

Slow- Fast Inference : Training-Free Inference Acceleration via Within-Sentence Support Stability

Xingyu Xie, Zhaochen Yu, Yue Liao, Tao Wang, Kim-Chuan Toh, Shuicheng Yan · arxiv.org · inference optimize llm faster inference throughput - new results
Why this rank: Scores 82 for the novel observation that attention support remains stable within sentences but shifts at semantic boundaries, which motivates an elegant event-driven sparse decoding framework. The principled Selector design with reverse-KL fusion and the system co-design achieving up to 14.36× speedup at 128K context while actually improving quality on LongBench represents a rare combination of theoretical insight and practical impact. The training-free nature makes it immediately deployable, and the speedup scaling with context length targets precisely the most pressing inference bottleneck.
Show full summary & analysis
Problem & Motivation: Long-context autoregressive decoding is computationally expensive because each decoding step must perform attention over a growing KV cache history. While KV caching avoids recomputing key/value projections, the attention computation itself still scales with context length. Existing sparse KV-cache compression methods either statically compress the cache after prefill or apply continuous per-step eviction/retrieval rules, but do not exploit a key temporal pattern: within a sentence or short semantically coherent span, the dominant attention support (the set of positions receiving most attention mass) tends to remain largely stable, with significant support shifts occurring primarily at semantic boundaries. This observation motivates an event-driven decoding strategy rather than treating every step identically. Methodology: The paper proposes Slow-Fast Inference (SFI), a training-free decoding framework that decomposes generation into two alternating modes. Fast steps attend only to a compact sparse cache consisting of sink tokens (global anchors), a recent sliding window (local continuity), and a set of selected long-range tokens (reused across steps). Slow steps are triggered at sentence/segment boundaries (detected via tokenizer-level punctuation/separator token IDs) or when a maximum refresh budget T_max is exceeded; they perform full dense attention over the entire accessible history and invoke a Selector to refresh the selected memory for subsequent fast steps. The Selector converts slow-step dense attention logits into updated sparse indices through a principled pipeline: (1) evidence aggregation via power-mean statistics over a short query window; (2) a cache-aware prior combining a key-norm downweighting factor (to suppress spuriously high-attention large-norm keys) and a position factor (global decay plus tail brake to prevent over-concentration near recent tokens); (3) reverse-KL fusion of evidence and prior via a convex combination with closed-form solution s_lambda = (1-lambda)*f + lambda*r, where lambda is chosen automatically by minimizing the L2 norm of the fused score to encourage distributional spread; (4) log-score refinements including Soft-NMS (suppressing locally non-maximal candidates within each head) and cross-head exclusivity (softly discouraging multiple heads from selecting the same position); (5) Top-K discretization to produce the final sparse index set. On the system side, SFI addresses two bottlenecks: (a) slow-step overhead is hidden via a layer-wise asynchronous pipeline where Selector execution and cache reorganization for layer i overlap with main-stream attention computation for layer i+1; (b) sparse attention bandwidth inefficiency from scattered reads is resolved by reorganizing selected KV pairs into a contiguous compact buffer after each slow step, enabling coalesced sequential memory reads for subsequent fast steps. Key Results: Across Qwen3 models (0.6B to 235B) and context lengths (8K to 128K), SFI consistently outperforms the full-KV baseline in decoding throughput: Qwen3-4B achieves 1.91× at 8K rising to 14.36× at 128K; Qwen3-30B-A3B achieves 1.66× to 11.98×; Qwen3-235B-A22B achieves 1.60× to 13.49×. At the kernel level, sparse attention with only 1.6% token retention achieves 10.67× speedup over dense attention at KV length 16K. On LongBench-V1, SFI matches or improves the full-KV baseline: Qwen3-4B improves average score from 41.40 to 43.19 (+1.8), Qwen3-30B-A3B from 46.91 to 48.13 (+1.2). On LongBench-V2, the 235B model improves the Long subset from 47.6 to 52.4 (+4.8). Against representative training-free KV-cache compression baselines (StreamingLLM, TOVA, SnapKV, ChunkKV, etc.) at 50% compression ratio, SFI achieves the best average score (34.80) while retaining only 15-20% of tokens—the only method to surpass the full-KV baseline. For long-CoT reasoning on GPQA and MMLU with Qwen3 Thinking checkpoints, SFI matches full-KV at medium and large scales (80.80/80.80 on GPQA for 235B). Strengths: - Training-free and directly applicable to existing checkpoints without any fine-tuning, making it immediately practical for deployed models. - The closed-form Selector with reverse-KL fusion provides a principled, theoretically motivated mechanism for combining attention evidence with structural priors, avoiding heuristic score aggregation. - The system co-design (asynchronous pipeline + memory-coalesced two-segment kernel) is essential for translating algorithmic sparsity into real wall-clock speedup, addressing a common gap between theoretical FLOPs reduction and practical gains. - Speedup advantage grows with context length, precisely targeting the most burdensome inference regime (long-context, long-CoT, agentic workloads). Weaknesses / Limitations: - The trigger policy relies on tokenizer-level boundary detection (punctuation, separators), which is language and tokenizer-specific and may not generalize well to languages without clear sentence-boundary tokens or to structured outputs (code, JSON) where semantic boundaries do not align with sentence punctuation. - The evaluation is conducted exclusively on Qwen3 model family; generalization of both quality and throughput results to other model architectures (e.g., Llama, Mistral, Gemma) is not demonstrated, leaving open questions about architectural sensitivity of the within-sentence support stability observation.

Revealing Behavioral Plasticity in Large Language Models: A Token-Conditional Perspective

Liyuan Mao, Le Yu, Jing Zhou, Chujie Zheng, Bowen Yu, Chang Gao, Shixuan Liu, An Yang, Weinan Zhang, JunYang Lin · arxiv.org · Recommended articles
Why this rank: This paper scores 82 for its elegant conceptual framing of behavioral plasticity as an intrinsic latent property of LLMs, exposable via a surprisingly simple token-conditional generation mechanism. The theoretical connection between KL divergence minimization and policy gradient objectives is rigorous, and the method achieves what all baselines fail to do: simultaneous improvement on both factual retrieval and complex math reasoning. The transferability of the discovered recalibrative reasoning pattern via SFT distillation adds practical significance beyond the core insight.
Show full summary & analysis
Problem & Motivation: Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) excel at complex mathematics via step-by-step chain-of-thought reasoning but perform significantly worse than instruction-tuned counterparts on factual question answering—Qwen3-30B-A3B-2507-Thinking scores 18.9 on SimpleQA versus 22.1 for its Instruct version. The root cause is that extended exploratory reasoning introduces unnecessary associations and unverified information that hinder direct knowledge retrieval. Existing behavioral steering methods (SFT, RLHF, DPO) merely amplify behaviors already present in training data, while prompt engineering lacks stable control. This paper identifies an orthogonal phenomenon: LLMs possess intrinsic behavioral plasticity—latent capabilities that can be exposed at inference time without any parameter modification, and subsequently stabilized via RL. Methodology: The paper introduces two complementary contributions. First, token-conditional generation: by prepending a short prefix (k≈6 tokens) sampled from the initial tokens of an instruction-tuned model's direct answer to a reasoning model's generation context, the LRM seamlessly shifts from step-by-step reasoning to direct knowledge retrieval. This exploits the autoregressive property that initial tokens establish a behavioral trajectory the model naturally continues. Second, ToCoRL (Token-Conditional Reinforcement Learning) internalizes this transient adaptation into persistent model behavior. The RL objective is a KL-constrained REINFORCE update where the reference policy π_TC is the token-conditional induced policy (a mixture of the base policy and the token-guided policy). A key theoretical result (Theorem 4.2) shows that minimizing KL(π_TC‖π_θ) shares the same gradient as a weighted policy-gradient objective, making the approach tractable. During training, rollouts are sampled from a mixed policy π_mix that stochastically interleaves normal generation and token-conditional generation, reducing variance while ensuring the target behavior is continuously reinforced. Training uses 10K factual query-answer pairs plus 10K math queries jointly, with binary rewards from an LLM-as-a-judge (gpt-4o-2024-08-06 for SimpleQA, gemini-2.5-flash for AA-Omniscience). Key Results: Starting from Qwen3-30B-A3B-2507-Thinking, ToCoRL improves SimpleQA from 18.9 to 28.3 (+9.4) and AA-Omniscience from 16.1 to 18.6, while simultaneously preserving or improving math: AIME'24 84.8→89.0, AIME'25 80.5→81.5. All baselines fail to achieve this dual improvement: GRPO reaches 23.6/16.7 on SimpleQA/Omniscience but maintains math (89.5/81.4); Adaptive-Thinking reaches 23.9/13.8 with math degradation (87.8/78.9); Instruct-Tuning GRPO reaches 21.1/14.8 (87.8/80.4). The emergent behavior—dubbed recalibrative reasoning—has the model begin with a direct answer attempt then iteratively enumerate candidate answers using minimal focused context until reaching confidence, distinct from both pure direct answering and associative step-by-step reasoning. Training dynamics show average response length rapidly decreases initially (elimination of spurious reasoning), then minimum length stays low while mean increases as the model learns to extend reasoning only when the first attempt fails. Ablations confirm robustness: performance varies by less than 1 point across λ∈{0.5,1,2} and k∈{6,7,8}, and the emergent behavior is nearly identical across three different prefix providers (235A22B-Instruct, 30A3B-Instruct, 2.5-14B). Starting from the base model (Qwen3-30A3B-Base with SFT initialization), ToCoRL still achieves 28.1 on SimpleQA versus 22.6/24.8/21.7 for baselines. The discovered behavior transfers via SFT: using ToCoRL-generated responses as SFT data yields 29.1 on SimpleQA, outperforming SFT data from the instruct models (28.3 and 25.3). Strengths: - Elegant conceptual framing: formalizes behavioral plasticity as a latent property exposable via token-conditional generation, supported by rigorous theoretical proofs connecting KL divergence to policy gradient objectives - Achieves simultaneous improvement on both factual retrieval and complex math—a capability trade-off all competing baselines fail to navigate - Strong transferability: the discovered behavioral pattern can be distilled into SFT data, enabling other models to acquire the behavior without additional RL training - Robust ablations across hyperparameters, model sizes, and training starting points (Thinking model and base model) Weaknesses / Limitations: - Experimental scope is narrow: the paper explicitly focuses on a single test case (factual QA + math reasoning on Qwen3 series) and acknowledges it does not evaluate ToCoRL on broader task types - The recalibrative reasoning pattern can produce extremely long reasoning chains for hard factual problems (the enumeration process can be extensive), which may be computationally prohibitive at inference time for deployed systems - Reward signal depends on LLM-as-a-judge quality; the paper notes less capable judge models can misclassify long incorrect responses as correct, introducing reward hacking risk

Vision-Language Models Encode Clinical Guidelines for Concept-Based Medical Reasoning

Mohamed Harmanani, Bining Long, Zhuoxin Guo, Paul F. R. Wilson, Amirhossein Sabour, Minh Nguyen Nhat To, Gabor Fichtinger, Purang Abolmaesumi, Parvin Mousavi · arxiv.org · karypis - new results
Why this rank: This paper scores 79 for its creative three-stage framework that elegantly bridges the gap between concept bottleneck models and clinical guideline reasoning. The guideline-driven concept enrichment strategy is a genuinely novel idea that transforms noisy binary annotations into rich clinical narratives, and the integration of structured clinical guidelines into the reasoning module meaningfully reduces hallucination compared to unconstrained VLMs. Strong empirical validation across three diverse datasets (including out-of-domain generalization to CUB-200) demonstrates the approach's robustness. The main limitation preventing a higher score is the reliance on costly concept annotations at training time and the added complexity/cost of the LRM inference component.
Show full summary & analysis
Problem & Motivation: Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) offer interpretable AI by mapping visual features to human-understandable concepts before making predictions. However, standard CBMs fall short in complex medical imaging tasks because they treat concept labels as discrete, noise-free signals and assume diagnosis is a deterministic function of concept presence. In practice, clinical reasoning requires broader contextual knowledge—such as how combinations of imaging findings map to BI-RADS risk categories—which is encoded in diagnostic guidelines rather than individual concept labels. Furthermore, concept annotations in medical datasets are often noisy or incomplete due to inter-observer variability, further weakening the concept-to-diagnosis link. Existing approaches lack a mechanism to ground concept predictions in structured clinical knowledge and to produce guideline-consistent explanations. Methodology: The paper introduces MedCBR, a three-stage framework. (1) Guideline-Driven Concept Enrichment: A large vision-language model (LVLM) is prompted with each image, its positive concept labels, the ground-truth diagnostic class, and a clinical guideline (e.g., BI-RADS atlas sections) to generate a structured textual report. This transforms sparse binary concept annotations into rich, guideline-conditioned narrative descriptions that capture relational and contextual meaning. (2) Vision-Language Concept Modelling: A CLIP-based model is fine-tuned with a multi-task objective combining (i) symmetric InfoNCE contrastive loss between image and enriched report embeddings, (ii) cross-entropy diagnostic classification via a linear head on image features, and (iii) per-concept cross-entropy prediction via lightweight two-layer MLP adapters. This jointly grounds visual representations in clinical semantics while maintaining concept-level interpretability. (3) Concept-Based Clinical Reasoning: At inference, a frozen large reasoning model (LRM) receives a structured prompt containing the CLIP model's predicted malignancy probability, per-concept confidence scores, and the relevant clinical guideline. The LRM interprets each concept's diagnostic implication against the guideline and generates a step-by-step natural language explanation with a BI-RADS assignment and recommended next steps. Key Results: On BUS-BRA (breast ultrasound, 1,875 images), MedCBR achieves 94.2% AUROC and 89.0% balanced accuracy, outperforming the best CLIP baseline (ViT-L/14: 93.5% / 88.1%) and best CBM variant (CLIP CBM: 91.8% / 86.4%). On CBIS-DDSM (mammography, ~3,500 ROIs), it achieves 84.0% AUROC and 76.4% balanced accuracy, exceeding CBM (+4.4% AUROC) and PCBM-h (+6.7%). On CUB-200-2011 (external validation with 11,788 bird images), MedCBR generalizes to 86.1% accuracy, outperforming the label-free CBM by over 10%. At the concept level, MedCBR achieves the highest per-concept AUROC across both medical datasets compared to standard CBMs and BiomedCLIP. For reasoning quality evaluated by a trained radiologist on 20 cases, MedCBR attains CIntS=95.4/95.2%, CIgS=98.3/87.5%, and BAS=86.0/80.0% on BUS-BRA/CBIS-DDSM—outperforming all VLM baselines (Llama 3.2, Qwen2.5, MedGemma, CLIP+Qwen3) on F1 (84.3/74.4%) while maintaining high sensitivity (82.5/78.7%) and specificity (94.3/76.0%). Ablations confirm that each component (contrastive loss, multi-task formulation, guideline conditioning) contributes incrementally to performance. Strengths: - The guideline-driven enrichment strategy elegantly addresses noisy/incomplete concept annotations by leveraging an LVLM to generate richer supervision signals, improving both concept detection and downstream classification without requiring additional manual annotation. - The reasoning module is explicitly conditioned on structured clinical guidelines and model-predicted concepts, substantially reducing hallucination risk compared to unconstrained VLM generation—as evidenced by consistently higher specificity and concept integration scores than off-the-shelf medical VLMs. - Demonstrated generalization beyond medical imaging (CUB-200) validates that the framework's benefits stem from principled multi-modal supervision rather than domain-specific shortcuts. Weaknesses / Limitations: - The framework requires annotated concept labels at training time, which are costly to obtain at scale; the authors acknowledge this as a key limitation and note that label-free extensions are left for future work. - The LRM component adds inference-time computational overhead and introduces dependence on large external models (e.g., GPT-class LRMs), which may complicate clinical deployment and raise cost/latency concerns not quantitatively addressed in the paper.

Speculative Decoding Scaling Laws (SDSL): Throughput Optimization Made Simple

Amirhossein Bozorgkhoo, Igor Molybog · arxiv.org · inference optimize llm faster inference throughput - new results
Why this rank: Scores 79 for providing the first closed-form analytical framework connecting pre-training scaling law hyperparameters to speculative decoding throughput. The discovery that the optimal draft-to-target size ratio converges to approximately 1/370 is both elegant and practically actionable, eliminating the need for expensive empirical draft model search. The rigorous demonstration that dataset size effects are negligible second-order corrections substantially simplifies the practical sizing rule. Wall-clock validation on real hardware strengthens the theoretical contribution.
Show full summary & analysis
Problem & Motivation: Speculative decoding accelerates LLM inference by using a small draft model to propose candidate tokens that a larger target model then verifies in parallel, yielding significant throughput gains without sacrificing output quality. However, the technique's effectiveness is highly sensitive to the choice of draft model: a poorly chosen draft introduces latency bottlenecks that can negate or reverse any speedup. Prior work selects draft models through expensive empirical search and benchmarking across multiple architectures, requiring substantial compute and research overhead. This paper addresses the gap by developing an analytical framework—Speculative Decoding Scaling Laws (SDSL)—that connects pre-training scaling law hyperparameters directly to inference throughput, enabling principled, pre-training-time selection of the optimal draft model size without additional empirical search. Methodology: The framework proceeds in three stages. First, the authors establish that the expected token acceptance rate α (the probability a draft token is accepted by the target model) can be modeled as an affine function of the perplexities of the draft and target models: α = Ax + By + C, where x is draft perplexity and y is target perplexity. This relationship is empirically validated across OPT, Qwen1.5, Qwen2.5, LLaMA3, LLaMA3.1, and Seed-OSS model families on HellaSwag. Second, this affine α model is substituted into a closed-form throughput expression (in tokens per FLOP) derived from prior work, yielding a throughput formula parameterized solely by model size M, draft size N, and training dataset sizes D and D'. Third, using the Chinchilla/Hoffmann pre-training scaling law to express perplexity as a function of N and D, the authors derive throughput T entirely in terms of (M, N, D, D'). The throughput-optimal draft size N* is found numerically via grid search over logarithmically spaced (N, D, M, D') values, then fit analytically to the leading-order linear relation N*(M) = μM + M₀. Regression uses OLS with HC3 heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors over 288 observations. Key Results: The affine fit for α achieves R² = 0.60 across all model families and draft/target pairings (MSE = 0.00128), with draft model perplexity being the dominant explanatory variable—target model perplexity contributes only weakly and inconsistently. The throughput-optimal draft-to-target size ratio N*/M converges asymptotically to μ ≈ 2.71 × 10⁻³ (roughly 1/370), meaning the optimal draft model is approximately 200× smaller than the target in large-model regimes—consistent with empirical practice in the literature. The full regression of N*/M = μ + M₀/M + γ log D + γ' log D' achieves R² = 0.978, with dataset size effects (γ, γ') entering only as statistically marginal second-order corrections; target training data size D' is statistically indistinguishable from zero.
